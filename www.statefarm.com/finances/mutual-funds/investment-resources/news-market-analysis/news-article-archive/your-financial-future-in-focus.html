Your Financial Future in Focus

Concentrating on these key influencers can help increase your nest egg

Are you ready for retirement? According to Gallup's annual Economy and Personal Finance Poll, 59% of Americans are worried they won't have enough money for retirement. Even if you are confident in your financial plan, the ever-changing economy, life's developments as well as career path fluctuations all demand that you make regular adjustments to help today's dollars last through tomorrow's retirement.

"It's not just developing a plan that's important — it's implementing it over time," says Grant Basting, a Home Office State Farm Advanced Planning Consultant. "As your resources increase, your savings need to increase."

Consider these factors and strategies to help you save smarter and live more confidently in retirement:

Automate Your Savings

Why it matters: You've guaranteed to pay yourself each month — no excuses. The amount should increase as your earnings do. "Putting a little more into savings can make a big difference in the long run," Basting says. "Compounding is money earning money, like a snowball rolling down a hill."

How to do it: Basting suggests an initial savings goal of 5% of your gross income, and working up to 10% as quickly as possible. If you earn more than $120,000, you may consider aiming higher with a goal of saving 20%.

Take advantage of any employer-defined contribution plans such as a 401(k). It's an easy way to automate savings each time you receive a paycheck, plus it grows income-tax- deferred. If your employer does not offer a retirement plan, consider a Traditional or Roth IRA with automatic monthly contributions.

Invest Wisely

Why it matters: Putting all your eggs in one basket is never a good strategy. That's why diversification, defined time horizon and risk tolerance are so important.

"Make sure your investments are working for you appropriately," Basting says. "Focus on an asset allocation consistent with the risk you are willing to accept — one that's comfortable for you and allows you to sleep at night."

How to do it: Regularly review your holdings and make adjustments as your life changes. For example, you may be comfortable with more risk when you're younger. But as you age, you may want to be more conservative. Checking in regularly with your agent can help you monitor your progress and implement any necessary adjustments.

Consider Taxation

Why it matters: We all have to pay taxes, but you can spread them strategically. Again: "Don't put all of your assets in one bucket," Basting says. "Otherwise, when you retire, you'll be at the mercy of the tax code." Instead, "Diversify your tax burden so you have tax options in retirement."

How to do it: Commonly utilized retirement strategies are typically divided between two buckets: pretax and after-tax. How do you decide what goes where? Consider the implications: With a traditional IRA/401(k), contributions are typically pretax, so taxes are owed when funds are withdrawn. With a Roth IRA/Roth 401(k), contributions are after-tax, so proceeds are tax-free as long as you follow applicable IRS guidelines regarding qualified distributions.

Factor in Inflation

Why it matters: "You won't be able to live the lifestyle you're accustomed to when you retire unless you save for the increased cost of goods and services," Basting says. "If you don't manage your resources, inflation will erode your purchasing power in the future."

How to do it: "Continuing to set aside a consistent percentage of income (with an initial goal of 5%) rather than a fixed amount will help your retirement savings grow along with inflation over time," Basting says. "As income increases, your annual savings will also increase."

Managing current expenses can also help keep you from paying their increased costs later. Start by considering essential and nonessential costs and minimize the latter. But don't stop there.

"Budgeting is important, but sitting down and discussing your finances with a financial representative helps keep your goals in focus," Basting says. "Everyone wants to save, but most people struggle to get started and stay focused."

Prepare for the Unexpected

Why it matters: An emergency fund dedicated to unplanned events, such as disability, unemployment and other emergencies, helps protect your retirement savings from pressing expenses. "You need to allow for these events, or they may derail your plan," Basting says.

How to do it: Consider an initial goal of setting aside $1,000, then continue making contributions until it accounts for at least six months of monthly net income. Ideally, you may wish to utilize a separate savings account or another accessible, secure account for emergency savings.

Resist Temptation

Why it matters: Staying focused on your long-term plan — instead of satisfying present-moment nonessential desires — means you'll have the resources to live comfortably in retirement.

How to do it: Basting admits: "It's not easy. It never has been. But discipline, dedication and willpower can help you stay the course." If you have questions about these strategies or how they apply to you, contact your State Farm agent.

Action Steps

  • Automate Your Savings: Aim to set aside 5% of your gross income each month.
  • Invest Wisely: Diversify your portfolio — and check back often.
  • Consider Taxation: Also diversify your tax burden among pretax, tax free, and after-tax vehicles.
  • Factor in Inflation: Save a consistent percentage of your income — rather than a fixed amount — to help manage rising costs.
  • Prepare for the Unexpected: Aim for an emergency fund equal to (or exceeding) six months' net income.
  • Resist Temptation: Stay focused on your long-term plan.
